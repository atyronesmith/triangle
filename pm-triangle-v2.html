The Iron Triangle in the Age of AI

Two honest positions exist on this question. The skeptical view says AI shifts the frontier but doesn't break the constraints. The bull case says AI is qualitatively different from every prior productivity tool. This page lets you stress-test both — and decide where on the spectrum the truth lives.

The case that AI actually is different

Bull case

AI is not a faster typewriter. Every prior productivity tool — spreadsheets, IDEs, CI/CD pipelines — accelerated existing human work. AI does something categorically different: it performs cognitive work that previously required additional humans. When a tool can draft, reason, and iterate on its own output, you haven't sped up the assembly line. You've added workers who don't need salaries, don't get tired, and don't call in sick.

Marginal cost of iteration approaches zero. Generating five drafts of a design doc, ten variants of a function, or twenty test cases costs nearly the same as generating one. This fundamentally changes the scope/quality tradeoff. In the old world, more iterations meant more time and cost. In the new world, iteration is effectively free. That's not shifting the frontier — it's changing the shape of the production function.

AI handles the "boring middle." Boilerplate, scaffolding, documentation, test generation, data transformation — these tasks consumed enormous human time without adding differentiated value. Freeing humans entirely from commodity cognitive work may genuinely break the old constraints because you're not optimizing the same process. You're eliminating entire categories of work.

The review burden is temporary. Current models hallucinate. Current teams are still learning to prompt effectively. But model capability is improving on a steep curve. The overhead you're measuring today is an adoption tax, not a permanent cost. Budgeting for 2024-level review costs in a 2026 plan is like budgeting for horse-feeding costs after you bought a car.

Historical precedent supports discontinuity. Spreadsheets didn't just make accounting faster — they made entirely new analyses possible that nobody would have attempted manually. The assembly line didn't just speed up car production — it made cars affordable to the people building them. These were genuine paradigm shifts where the old constraint models stopped applying. AI may be another.

Skeptic's response

Every technology generation has made this claim. Mainframes were going to eliminate middle management. PCs were going to create the paperless office. Agile was going to make estimation unnecessary. The cloud was going to make infrastructure free. In every case, the constraints reasserted themselves — they just moved to different places. AI will be the same.

Zero-cost iteration is an illusion. Generating five drafts is cheap. Evaluating five drafts to pick the best one and verify it's correct is not. You've traded production cost for evaluation cost. Someone still has to review, decide, and take responsibility. That someone is human and expensive.

The "boring middle" was load-bearing. The reason documentation and tests existed wasn't because humans enjoyed writing them. It was because the process of writing them forced understanding. Skip the process, and you get teams that ship AI-generated code they don't fully understand, with AI-generated tests that don't cover the actual failure modes.

"The models will get better" is a bet, not a plan. You can't manage a project on the assumption that your tools will improve mid-project. And even if models improve, the tasks you give them will get harder too — that's how organizations work. You solve the easy problems, then the hard ones become the new baseline.

Spreadsheets prove the triangle, not disprove it. Spreadsheets made new analyses possible — and then organizations demanded more analyses, faster, from fewer people. The constraints didn't disappear. They scaled up. Forty years later, finance teams are still overworked and under-resourced. The frontier moved. The triangle held.

Where this leaves you

The honest answer is: nobody knows yet

Both positions are internally consistent and supported by evidence. The skeptic points to every prior technology cycle and notes that constraints always reassert. The optimist points to the qualitative difference between automating physical work and automating cognitive work, and argues this time really might be different.

What's not honest is pretending the question is settled. An organization that mandates "use AI everywhere to increase output" without acknowledging the uncertainty is making a bet and calling it a strategy. The triangle is a useful forcing function: it makes you name which constraint you're relaxing and which you're holding. If your leadership can't answer that question, the triangle is doing its job — and it's not obsolete.

The pragmatic position: act like the skeptic, hope for the optimist. Budget for real overhead. Measure actual vs. theoretical gains. Build review processes. And if the models get so good that review becomes unnecessary — great, you'll have slack in the budget. That's a better failure mode than the reverse.
