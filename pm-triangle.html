What this model shows

AI is a frontier shifter, not a constraint eliminator

The project management triangle — scope, time, cost, with quality as a function of all three — has been around since the 1950s. The core insight is simple: "Good, fast, cheap. Choose two." Changes in one constraint necessitate changes in others, or quality suffers.

AI genuinely expands what's achievable within a given time/cost envelope. But it doesn't eliminate the constraint relationships. What it does is create an illusion that the constraints have been removed — which is exactly when organizations make their worst planning decisions.

The hidden cost gap

The gap between the blue triangle (theoretical AI capacity) and the red triangle (actual achievable output) represents costs that rarely appear on a line item: prompt engineering time, output validation overhead, hallucination remediation, integration testing of AI-generated code, and the organizational learning curve. These are real even if nobody budgets for them.

The scope-push trap

When leadership sees AI productivity gains, the typical response isn't "let's use this headroom for quality." It's "now do 2x the work." The amber dashed triangle — management's expected output — races ahead of what's actually achievable. The gap between amber and red is where quality degrades: missed reviews, unvalidated output, and technical debt nobody planned for.

Review is the hidden constraint

Try the "sweet spot" preset, then lower review effort. Quality collapses. AI-generated output requires proportionally more review than human-generated output, not less — because the failure modes are different and harder to spot. Code that compiles and passes tests but has subtle architectural problems. Text that reads fluently but contains fabricated citations. The review budget is the new binding constraint that most organizations refuse to acknowledge.
